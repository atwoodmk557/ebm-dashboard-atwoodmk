## Study 1: Coaching Transition and Nature of Change (Johnson, Elam, & Matz, 2024)

**Full Citation:**
Johnson, J., Elam, N., & Matz, D. (2024). Coaching transition and nature of change: An analysis of NCAA Division I coaching changes from 2010-2020. *Journal of Intercollegiate Sport, 17*(2). https://doi.org/10.17161/jis.v17i2.21131

**Study Overview:**
- **Population:** NCAA Division I head coaches across all sports
- **Sample Size:** n = 414 coaching changes (2010-2020)
- **Design:** Cross-sectional correlational study with binary logistic regression
- **Key Finding:** 53.9% of changes were negative; program winning percentage strongest predictor (r = .18)

### CAT MANAGER APPRAISAL QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

**Q1: This study is a ____**
- **Answer:** Cross-sectional study
- **Rationale:** Takes a "snapshot" of 414 coaching changes from 2010-2020; looks at relationships between variables at one point in time; no tracking of same teams over time

**Q2: Was the sample randomly selected?**
- **Answer:** NO
- **Rationale:** Used all available/identified coaching changes from 2010-2020 (convenience/census sample, not random selection from larger pool); typical for observational sports research
- **Impact:** Possible selection bias but large sample size helps compensate

**Q3: Was the sample size large enough?**
- **Answer:** YES - Excellent
- **Rationale:** n = 414 provides >95% power to detect small-medium effects; far exceeds minimum requirements for regression (need 10-15 per predictor × 8 predictors = 120 minimum); much larger than typical sport management studies

**Q4: Is it unlikely that data dredging has taken place?**
- **Answer:** YES - Low risk
- **Rationale:** Clear hypothesis stated upfront; theory-driven variable selection; reasonable number of tests (6-8 correlations); most significant at p<.01 (not borderline); peer-reviewed publication
- **Minor concern:** No multiple comparison correction mentioned, but not a major red flag

**Q5: Were reliable and valid measurement methods used?**
- **Answer:** YES - Good quality
- **Rationale:**
  * Winning percentage: Objective, verifiable, standardized (excellent reliability & validity)
  * APR scores: Official NCAA metric, highly reliable and valid
  * Coaching tenure: Objective count of months (excellent)
  * Nature of change (positive/negative): Clear operational definitions (good)
- **Assessment:** All key variables use objective, standardized, publicly available data with minimal bias

**Q6: How large was the effect size?**
- **Answer:** SMALL to SMALL-MEDIUM effects
- **Effect Sizes:**
  * Pearson r = .18 (strongest correlation) = Between small (.10) and medium (.30) per Cohen
  * Nagelkerke RÂ² = .146 (14.6% variance) = Medium effect for variance explained
- **Practical Significance:** Modest but meaningful for organizational research; performance explains ~1/7 of coaching change decisions (85% due to other factors like politics, budget, personality)

- **Answer:** UNKNOWN - Precision NOT reported

## Study 3: Managerial Change, Longevity, and Organizational Effectiveness (Eitzen & Yetman, 1972)

**Full Citation:**
Eitzen, D.S., & Yetman, N.R. (1972). Managerial change, longevity, and organizational effectiveness. *Administrative Science Quarterly, 17*(1), 110-116.

**Study Overview:**
- **Population:** NCAA college basketball teams
- **Sample Size:** n = 129 schools, 657 coaching changes, 2,114 year-to-year comparison observations
- **Time Period:** 1930-1970 (40 years)
- **Design:** Non-randomized controlled study (longitudinal with COMPARISON GROUPS)
- **Key Finding:** "Coaching shifts do not affect performance" (regression to mean); coaching stability optimal at 10-12 years; decline after 13+ years

---

### CAT MANAGER APPRAISAL QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

**Q1: This study is a ____**
- **Answer:** Non-randomized controlled study
- **Rationale:** Has comparison/control groups (teams WITH vs WITHOUT coaching changes) but no random assignment; quasi-experimental design tracking natural variation over 40 years; compares outcomes between groups while controlling for baseline differences

**Q2: Was the control group similar to the intervention group at the start of the study?**
- **Answer:** YES …
- **Rationale:**
  * Same population: All NCAA college basketball teams (1930-1970)
  * Same time period: All tracked simultaneously over 40 years
  * Natural variation: Some teams had coaching changes, some didn't - no pre-selection bias
  * **Controlled for regression to the mean:** Used 2,114 comparison observations to account for natural performance fluctuations
  * Baseline equivalence: Both groups were college basketball programs with similar organizational structures
- **This is a MAJOR strength** - proper comparison group design allows causal inference

**Q3: Did the study use a before and after measurement?**
- **Answer:** YES …
- **Rationale:**
  * Longitudinal tracking year-to-year over 40 years (1930-1970)
  * **Before measurement:** Team's winning percentage in year(s) BEFORE coaching change
  * **After measurement:** Team's winning percentage in year(s) AFTER coaching change
  * Compared change in performance for teams WITH changes vs teams WITHOUT changes
  * This establishes **temporal precedence** - can see what happened first
- **Critical for causation:** Before-after design shows whether coaching changes precede performance changes or vice versa

**Q4: Did the study start prior to the intervention?**
- **Answer:** YES …
- **Rationale:**
  * Study began in 1930; coaching changes occurred throughout 1930-1970 period
  * Had baseline performance data already collected before changes occurred
  * Not retrospective - tracked events as they unfolded over 40 years
  * **Establishes temporal precedence:** Researchers weren't looking backwards after the fact
- **Strength:** Prospective longitudinal design captures natural timeline of events

**Q5: Were reliable and valid measurement methods used?**
- **Answer:** YES …
- **Rationale:**
  * **Coaching changes:** Objective, publicly verifiable events - clear operational definition (head coach changed or didn't)
  * **Winning percentage:** Official NCAA records - standardized calculation (games won Ã· games played), highly reliable, minimal measurement error
  * **Tenure length:** Years coached at same institution - objective count from public records
  * **40-year historical scope:** NCAA basketball well-established by 1930; records reliable and publicly available
  * All variables objective with no subjective ratings or self-report bias
- **Minor concern:** 1930s-1940s record-keeping may have been less precise than modern era, but NCAA Division I was already professionalized

**Q6: How large was the effect size?**
- **Answer:** MEDIUM
- **Effect Size:** r = -.28 (negative correlation)
  * Cohen's guidelines: Small r=.10, Medium r=.30, Large r=.50
  * r = .28 is at the **small-to-medium boundary, leaning medium** (only .02 from medium threshold)
  * rÂ² = .08 (8% of variance explained)
- **Interpretation:** Negative sign means higher coaching turnover â†’ lower winning percentage (performance decline)
- **Comparison to other studies:**
  * Study 1: r = .18 (small-medium)
  * Study 2: r = .65 (LARGE)
  * Study 3: r = -.28 (medium)
- **Important context:** Even though smaller than Study 2's effect, Study 3's **quasi-experimental design is much stronger** - sometimes smaller effects from better-designed studies are more trustworthy!

**Q7: How precise was the effect size?**
- **Answer:** UNKNOWN / NOT REPORTED
- **Rationale:**
  * No confidence intervals reported for r = -.28
  * No standard errors provided
  * Likely only reported p < .05 (typical for 1972 publication standards)
  * **Generational issue:** Confidence intervals weren't standard practice in 1972; became required in 2000s+ publications
- **Mitigating factor:** Massive sample size (n=129 schools, 657 changes, 2,771 total observations over 40 years) suggests reasonable precision even though not quantified
- **Impact:** Minor weakness given era; modern replication would include CIs
